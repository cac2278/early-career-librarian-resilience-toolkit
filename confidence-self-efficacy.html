Confidence & Self-Efficacy

When repeated instability begins reshaping your sense of capability

One of the most painful aspects of early-career librarianship is that instability rarely remains external.

Over time, repeated rejection, precarious employment, financial uncertainty, institutional ambiguity, and chronic evaluation can begin reshaping the way you experience yourself internally.

At first, confidence may feel temporarily shaken.

Eventually, you may begin questioning your competence, your legitimacy, your judgment, your future, or whether you ever understood your own abilities accurately at all.

You may become trapped between two contradictory realities: objective evidence of accomplishment, and the persistent emotional feeling that none of it is enough.

This page exists for people experiencing that contradiction.

Self-efficacy does not collapse simply because someone lacks talent or intelligence. Often, it deteriorates because people have spent too long surviving systems that repeatedly destabilize their sense of professional continuity, safety, and recognition.

Under conditions of chronic precarity, confidence itself can become difficult to sustain.

Use This Page If...

You no longer trust your own perception of your abilities. Rejection has started feeling like revelation rather than circumstance. You feel ashamed for still struggling despite your accomplishments.

You may also be here because you are exhausted by constantly proving yourself, you feel emotionally dependent on external validation, or you no longer know how to experience yourself outside institutional evaluation.

Confidence Under Neoliberalism

Neoliberal labor culture encourages people to experience confidence as an individual psychological achievement rather than something shaped by material conditions.

Workers are expected to self-motivate, self-optimize, self-brand, self-regulate, and remain perpetually employable regardless of structural instability.

Under these conditions, confidence becomes moralized.

If you struggle professionally, the problem is framed not as labor precarity, institutional contradiction, underfunding, or exploitative systems, but as insufficient resilience, adaptability, or self-belief.

The result is that many early-career librarians internalize instability as personal failure.

“If I were truly capable, I would already be stable.”

“If my accomplishments really mattered, someone would have hired me by now.”

“If I keep getting rejected, there must be something fundamentally wrong with me.”

But confidence does not emerge in a social vacuum.

Self-efficacy is profoundly shaped by recognition, stability, relational safety, mentorship, labor conditions, and repeated experiences of institutional trust.

Rejection and Epistemic Destabilization

Repeated professional rejection often becomes epistemologically disorienting.

At first, rejection may feel disappointing but containable.

Over time, however, people may begin questioning whether their accomplishments were ever meaningful, whether mentors or colleagues were simply being polite, whether they fundamentally misunderstood their own competence, or whether everyone else can already see something they cannot.

This is especially painful for intellectually reflective people who rely heavily on coherence and self-understanding.

You may find yourself compulsively replaying interviews, analyzing social interactions, revising your personality, comparing yourself to peers, or searching for the hidden flaw that explains your instability.

The danger is that rejection begins functioning not merely as an event, but as a total interpretive framework through which all previous experience becomes re-read.

Professional disappointment transforms into identity collapse.

Imposter Syndrome and Structural Reality

“Imposter syndrome” is often discussed as though it were purely an internal cognitive distortion.

But many people experience self-doubt because institutions genuinely produce instability, exclusion, surveillance, and uneven distributions of legitimacy.

Critical race theory, feminist theory, and critical disability studies remind us that some workers are systematically required to overperform competence, self-monitor constantly, code-switch, manage stereotype threat, and navigate institutions that were not built with them in mind.

Under these conditions, self-doubt may reflect not irrationality, but prolonged adaptation to environments where belonging genuinely feels conditional.

This does not mean every self-critical thought is accurate. But it does mean confidence cannot be reduced to mindset alone.

The Collapse of Self-Trust

One of the most devastating consequences of chronic professional instability is the gradual erosion of self-trust.

You may stop trusting your judgment, your ambitions, your emotional responses, your perception of relationships, or your ability to evaluate your own work accurately.

Instead, institutional outcomes begin functioning as the primary measure of reality.

A hiring committee becomes more authoritative than your lived experience. A rejection feels more real than years of demonstrated competence. Temporary employment begins to eclipse the evidence of everything you have already accomplished.

This is psychologically dangerous.

Because once self-trust collapses, people become increasingly vulnerable to overwork, emotional dependence on institutions, compulsive self-optimization, perfectionism, and despair.

Rebuilding confidence often begins not with positive thinking, but with recovering the ability to recognize reality outside institutional judgment.

Confidence Beyond Institutional Recognition

One of the central emotional challenges of precarious professional life is learning how to maintain selfhood while external recognition remains unstable.

This does not mean pretending recognition no longer matters. Jobs, stability, pay, healthcare, and institutional trust matter materially and psychologically.

But sustainable self-efficacy requires developing forms of self-recognition that are not entirely reducible to institutional outcomes.

This may involve learning to recognize meaningful labor before it is rewarded, growth before it is formally validated, competence before it becomes permanent employment, and humanity outside productivity altogether.

The goal is not invulnerability.

The goal is preventing institutions from becoming the sole authors of your reality.
