Job Searching & Rejection

When professional rejection begins feeling existential

There is a particular kind of exhaustion that emerges when job searching stops feeling temporary and starts feeling like a permanent condition of life.

You revise your materials repeatedly. You relocate mentally before relocation is materially possible. You rehearse interviews. You apply for jobs you are overqualified for and jobs you are underqualified for. You remain hopeful because hopelessness feels professionally dangerous.

At first, rejection feels disappointing.

Over time, repeated rejection can begin to destabilize your sense of self entirely.

You may begin wondering whether your accomplishments matter, whether your professional identity is real, whether everyone else can see something fundamentally lacking in you, or whether the profession simply has no place for you despite everything you have given to it.

These feelings intensify under conditions of precarity, especially when employment is temporary, financial stability feels distant, institutional recognition remains inconsistent, and professional belonging appears permanently conditional.

This page exists for people living inside that experience.

Use This Page If...

You are exhausted from applying for jobs. You feel ashamed after rejection. You are beginning to interpret non-selection as proof of inadequacy. You are struggling to continue believing in yourself while remaining professionally precarious.

You may also be here because you are surviving the uniquely painful experience of being an internal candidate — continuing to work inside the same institution while the search proceeds publicly around you.

Rejection Under Late Capitalism

Neoliberal labor culture encourages people to experience employability as a reflection of moral worth.

Under these conditions, rejection rarely feels administrative. It feels personal, epistemic, and existential.

“I was not selected.”

becomes

“I am not enough.”

Late capitalism intensifies this process by encouraging workers to treat themselves as projects requiring endless optimization. Every rejection becomes interpreted as evidence that one has failed to network correctly, market oneself effectively, perform confidence adequately, or become sufficiently employable.

The result is profound self-surveillance.

You may find yourself compulsively replaying interviews, analyzing social interactions, revising your personality, comparing yourself to finalists, or searching for the hidden flaw that explains why stability still feels unreachable.

What often disappears in this process is structural analysis.

Library hiring systems are shaped by austerity, underfunding, institutional politics, contingent labor structures, geographic inequality, and increasingly precarious employment conditions.

None of this removes the pain of rejection. But it complicates the fantasy that every outcome reflects pure merit.

Internal Rejection and Institutional Visibility

Internal rejection can be especially psychologically destabilizing because it transforms disappointment into a public environment you cannot leave.

Unlike external candidates, internal applicants often continue working while finalists are publicly welcomed, coworkers participate enthusiastically in the search, professional relationships become emotionally ambiguous, and institutional life proceeds visibly without them.

This can create a uniquely painful form of social exposure.

You may find yourself struggling to concentrate at work, interpreting ordinary interactions catastrophically, feeling ashamed for caring deeply, or becoming hyperaware of how visible your disappointment feels.

You may also experience relational destabilization: coworkers avoiding the subject, performing institutional normalcy, or failing to acknowledge the emotional reality of what you are experiencing.

This can feel profoundly alienating.

Especially because the institution often expects professionalism precisely at the moment your nervous system feels least capable of sustaining it.

Shame, Professionalism, and Emotional Containment

Professional culture often treats emotional containment as maturity.

As a result, many early-career librarians learn to experience visible disappointment as humiliation rather than grief.

You may feel pressure to remain composed, avoid “making things awkward,” continue performing enthusiasm, and minimize your own pain in order to preserve institutional comfort.

Under these conditions, shame flourishes.

Not because rejection itself is shameful, but because institutions frequently leave people emotionally isolated while expecting them to continue functioning normally.

“If I were truly competent, this would not have happened.”

“Everyone else can see that I failed.”

“I must have misjudged my own worth entirely.”

Acute rejection can also disrupt concentration, executive functioning, sleep, appetite, and emotional regulation.

These are not signs of weakness. They are common nervous-system responses to perceived social threat, instability, and grief.

Meritocracy, Legibility, and Institutional Power

One of the most painful aspects of repeated rejection is that it often begins to feel morally explanatory.

People want hiring systems to make sense. They want outcomes to feel coherent.

But institutional hiring is shaped by budgets, personalities, departmental politics, institutional anxiety, labor classifications, risk management, and often invisible forms of bias.

Critical race theory and intersectional feminism remind us that professional legitimacy is not distributed equally.

Some people are institutionally legible immediately. Others must repeatedly demonstrate competence while navigating racialized assumptions, gendered expectations, disability stigma, classed professionalism, and uneven access to mentorship, networks, and safety.

This does not mean individual preparation is irrelevant. But it does mean rejection cannot be understood solely as a transparent measure of worth.

Continuing After Rejection

One of the cruelest aspects of professional rejection is that life continues immediately afterward.

Emails still arrive. Projects remain unfinished. Coworkers continue socializing. Students still need help. Finalists visit campus. Work continues while your internal world feels destabilized.

Recovery rarely begins with confidence.

It often begins with stabilization: sleeping, eating, reducing shame, narrowing time horizons, completing small concrete tasks, and resisting the urge to transform one rejection into a total theory of yourself.

You do not need to convince yourself that rejection “doesn’t matter.”

You only need to resist allowing institutional outcomes to become the sole author of your identity.
