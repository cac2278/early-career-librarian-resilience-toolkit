Mentorship & Community

When professional survival becomes emotionally isolating

Many people enter librarianship hoping not only for meaningful work, but for intellectual community, mentorship, solidarity, and belonging.

The profession often presents itself as collaborative and care-centered. Conferences, graduate programs, committees, and workplaces frequently emphasize connection, collegiality, and shared values.

Sometimes these relationships become deeply meaningful.

But many early-career librarians also discover that professional life under precarity can become profoundly isolating.

Temporary contracts, geographic instability, repeated rejection, institutional competition, emotional exhaustion, and uneven power dynamics often make sustained community difficult to maintain.

Over time, this instability can produce a painful contradiction: you are surrounded by professional interaction, yet increasingly emotionally alone.

This page exists for people trying to build forms of connection and support that remain possible even under unstable institutional conditions.

Use This Page If...

You feel professionally isolated. You are exhausted by networking culture. You are struggling to distinguish mentorship from institutional usefulness.

You may also be here because you feel emotionally disconnected from your workplace, you are grieving professional relationships that did not survive institutional pressure, or you are beginning to realize that survival in librarianship often requires forms of community institutions themselves cannot reliably provide.

Precarity and Relational Instability

Many early-career librarians expect instability to affect employment. Fewer expect it to affect relationships.

Temporary appointments, contingent labor, relocation pressure, and chronic uncertainty make it difficult to build continuity over time. People move institutions. Contracts end. Mentors leave. Professional communities fracture.

Under these conditions, it can become difficult to determine who feels genuinely safe, which relationships are reciprocal, and what forms of vulnerability remain survivable.

The result is that many librarians begin carrying loneliness that is difficult to name.

Mentorship and Institutional Power

Mentorship is often discussed as though it were uncomplicated. In reality, mentorship exists within systems shaped by hierarchy, precarity, race, gender, disability, class, and institutional power.

Good mentorship can be transformative. It can help people survive difficult periods, understand institutional cultures, develop confidence, and imagine futures that once felt inaccessible.

But mentorship can also become complicated. Mentors may leave, become unavailable, have limited influence, disappoint us, or struggle to provide the support we hoped they could offer.

Many early-career librarians are searching not only for guidance, but for recognition. And recognition can feel difficult to replace when it disappears.

Networking, Performance, and Professional Exhaustion

Professional networking is often presented as an unquestionably positive activity. Yet many librarians experience networking as exhausting, especially under conditions of precarity.

You may feel pressure to remain enthusiastic, appear professionally confident, perform competence continuously, and transform every interaction into potential opportunity.

The goal subtly shifts from building relationships to maintaining employability.

This can be especially alienating for people who entered librarianship seeking intellectual companionship, collaboration, or genuine community.

Community Beyond Institutional Legibility

One of the most important lessons many librarians learn is that institutions cannot meet every relational need.

Institutions can provide employment, visibility, credentials, and collaboration. But they often struggle to provide continuity, emotional safety, reciprocity, or collective care.

As a result, sustainable professional life frequently depends upon building forms of community that are not entirely dependent upon institutional structures.

Community is not simply a professional resource. It is often a survival resource.

Grief, Disappointment, and Relational Rupture

One of the least discussed aspects of professional life is relational grief.

People often grieve mentors who became unavailable, coworkers who disappeared during difficult moments, communities that fractured, or relationships that felt more meaningful than they ultimately proved capable of sustaining.

These losses can feel surprisingly profound because they often carried hope, belonging, recognition, and emotional survival.

Professional disappointment is sometimes easier to understand than relational disappointment.

Further Reading

  • bell hooks — Communion (2002)
  • Pema Chödrön — When Things Fall Apart (1996)
  • Audre Lorde — Sister Outsider (1984)
  • Mariame Kaba — interviews on collective care and political hope

Beyond Professional Survival

One of the central emotional challenges of librarianship under precarity is learning how to remain open to connection without making institutional belonging the sole source of meaning.

Sustainable community often emerges when relationships become grounded not in competition, visibility, institutional usefulness, or advancement, but in mutual recognition.

The goal is not networking harder. The goal is building forms of connection capable of surviving beyond institutional instability.
