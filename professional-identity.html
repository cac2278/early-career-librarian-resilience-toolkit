Professional Identity

When professional life begins destabilizing your sense of self

There is a particular kind of pain that can emerge early in librarianship when you have done everything “correctly.” You worked hard. You excelled academically. You built a strong CV. You remained flexible. You accepted temporary work because you believed it would eventually lead somewhere stable.

And still, stability never fully arrived.

Over time, repeated precarity and rejection can begin to feel less like professional setbacks and more like existential verdicts. You may begin wondering whether the profession actually has a place for you at all. You may feel embarrassed for having hoped so much in the first place.

Professional identity in librarianship is not formed in isolation. It develops within institutions shaped by labor precarity, emotional extraction, vocational awe, neoliberal productivity culture, racialized and gendered expectations, and unequal distributions of legitimacy and institutional safety.

The result is that many early-career librarians quietly experience chronic self-monitoring, emotional fragmentation, and the terrifying feeling that they are becoming professionally invisible.

This is not an individual weakness or failure. It is a structural condition.

Use This Page If...

You are beginning to treat rejection as evidence about your worth. Your labor consistently exceeds your title, pay, or recognition. You feel emotionally exhausted by proving yourself repeatedly.

Professionalism itself has started to feel alienating. You no longer know whether you are trying to become a librarian or merely survive librarianship.

Professional Identity Under Precarity

Many early-career librarians live inside a profound contradiction: they are expected to perform professional confidence and institutional commitment while simultaneously being denied stability.

You may be asked to teach, mentor, support students, absorb emotional labor, run projects, and demonstrate institutional value while remaining temporary, contingent, and perpetually replaceable. Under these conditions, professional identity can become psychologically destabilized.

“I want to contribute meaningfully.”

can slowly transform into

“I must constantly prove I deserve to exist here.”

Under these conditions, professional identity can become increasingly organized around evaluation rather than growth. Professional energy that might otherwise be directed toward learning, experimentation, contribution, or long-term development becomes redirected toward proving competence, legitimacy, and employability.

The result is not simply exhaustion. It can become difficult to maintain a stable sense of who you are professionally when your place within the profession feels permanently uncertain.

Vocational Awe and the Moralization of Suffering

Fobazi Ettarh's concept of vocational awe helps explain why many librarians struggle to distinguish commitment from self-sacrifice.

When librarianship is framed as uniquely meaningful or socially valuable work, professional challenges can begin to take on moral significance. Exhaustion may feel like evidence of dedication. Boundaries may feel difficult to maintain. Instability may become something people feel they should tolerate because the work itself matters deeply.

“If I cared enough, I would be able to endure more.”

Under these conditions, it can become difficult to recognize when expectations have become unsustainable. People may begin interpreting structural problems as personal shortcomings or viewing chronic overextension as a normal part of professional life.

This toolkit rejects the idea that suffering is proof of professional legitimacy.

Professionalism, Whiteness, and Institutional Legibility

Critical race theory, intersectional feminism, and decolonial theory remind us that professionalism is not neutral.

Norms around composure, communication, collegiality, emotional expression, appearance, and institutional “fit” are shaped by histories of whiteness, colonialism, ableism, class privilege, and gendered expectations around emotional labor and self-management.

As a result, many workers — especially those marginalized by race, gender, disability, sexuality, class, or other intersecting identities — must continuously negotiate code-switching, hypervisibility, invisibility, institutional surveillance, and pressure toward self-erasure.

Professional legitimacy is not distributed equally. Some workers are presumed competent, while others must repeatedly perform competence while managing stereotypes and institutional scrutiny.

“Who am I becoming?”

becomes

“What parts of myself am I being asked to suppress in order to remain institutionally survivable?”

Emotional Labor and Invisible Extraction

Much library labor is affective labor. It involves attentiveness, warmth, emotional regulation, mentorship, de-escalation, accessibility work, collegial diplomacy, and maintaining calm under stress.

Because this labor is feminized and relational, institutions often treat it as personality rather than skill. Workers are expected to remain endlessly responsive while protecting institutional harmony regardless of personal exhaustion.

The result is a profession where many people feel emotionally depleted while struggling to explain why.

You may be burned out not because you are weak, but because your nervous system has spent years adapting to chronic emotional extraction.
